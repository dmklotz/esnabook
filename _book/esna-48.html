The Temple of Esna: a Continual Translation

Esna 48

  • Date: Unknown, probably Vespasian

  • Bibliography: Sauneron (2012), p. 74 (48 A, partial translation)

48 A: Bandeau South (left)

dgȝ Rʿ jm=f m ḫy šps
   ms m dwȝw
ẖnm=f jt=f Tȝ-ṯnn

Re is seen within as an august child,
   who is born in the morning.
He unites with his father, Tatenen,
   after he (Tatenen) lifted up the sky [bear]ing him,
       on III Peret (Phamenoth) 1.1
ḥr-ȝḫty sḥḏ tȝ.wy
   ʿnḫ(.w) n mȝwy=f
   wpš nn.t
   šnbtj nb hȝy.ty
       ḫprj pw rʿ-nb

Harakhty, who illumines the two lands,
   from whose rays (one) lives,
   who brightens the sky,
   falcon, lord of the double firmament,
       this one is Khepri, every day.
wsn pfy
jr nḏmnḏm
bȝ sṯj n fdw ḥr.w
    ḫnt nb-ʿnḫ

That one2 (Khnum-Tatenen) is the virile one,
he who creates sexual pleasure,
the engendering ram with four faces,3
   foremost of Neb-ankh.
sḥḏ.n=f ḥw.t-jt
    m pr=f
bẖ.n=f ḥw.t-ṯȝ.wy
    m wbn=f
    jrr sḫ.t m nqr

As he illumined the Temple of the Father
      by his coming forth,
so did he brighten the Temple of the Two Children
      by his rising,
   so that the field is overlaid4 with gold dust.
ḏ(d).tw js Jmn m rn=f
   wṯz.n=f p.t m hȝy.t n bȝ=f
   wḏ.n=f dwȝ.t r ḥȝp ḏ.t=f

He is moreover called “Amun” by name:5
   as he raised up the sky as a chapel for his Ba,
   so did he ordain the Duat to conceal his body.
nṯr pn šps
ẖnm-Rʿ nb-tȝ-sn.t
nṯr-ʿȝ nb sḫ.t

This august god,
Khnum-Re Lord of Esna,
Great god, Lord of the Field.

Notes

  1. The festival of “lifting up heaven” on Phamenoth 1 is widely attested since the New Kingdom, and most frequently involves the Memphite demiurge Ptah-Tatenen; cf. Berlandini (1995), pp. 29-31. At Esna, this coincided with the major festival of installing the potter’s wheel by Khnum-Re-Tatenen: Sauneron (2012), pp. 71-88, 81-244; Leitz and Löffler (2019).↩

  2. The text still seems to describe the union between the solar god (pw = Khepri), and his father, the demiurge and procreator (pfy = Tatenen). Nonetheless, Khnum is elsewhere called explicitly wsn pfy: [Esna II, 17], 6-7; [Esna III, 249], 2; similarly [Esna II, 184], 12; cf. Pichel (2018) pp. 22-23, n. i.↩

  3. Reference to the quadrifrontic ram of Mendes, composed of Re, Shu, Geb, and Osiris. They are explicitly labelled elsewhere in [Esna II 17], 46-49; [Esna III, 250], 16-17; [Esna III, 232], 6; separate depictions of them in [Esna II 140]. Cf. Pichel (2018), pp. 76-79. The Mendesian ram frequently receives these same virile epithets, as well as Khnum at Esna.↩

  4. Not listed in dictionaries, this verb () seems to be the same hieroglyphic word rr which occurs in divine epithets such as rr m nbw, “overlaid with gold”. It might be related to Demotic lʿlʿ, Coptic ⲗⲁⲗⲉ: Klotz (2015), pp. 91-93, n. m.↩

  5. Yet another reference to constructing a firmament (hȝy.t), this section appears to refer again to Tatenen, one aspect of Khnum-Re. The god Amun-Re is frequently praised for establishing all regions of the cosmos (cf. Deir Chelouit III, 125, 8-9).↩
